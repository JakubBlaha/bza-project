\begin{abstract}
    Knowledge editing methods such as ROME, MEMIT, AlphaEdit, and EMMET enable targeted injection of new facts into large language model weights without full retraining. Separately, post-training quantization is widely used to compress models for efficient deployment. However, the interaction between these two techniques remains unexplored: does quantization preserve or destroy knowledge that was injected via editing? In this paper, the impact of GPTQ quantization at 8, 4, 3, and 2-bit precision is systematically evaluated on models edited using AlphaEdit, MEMIT, and EMMET. Experiments are conducted on various LLMs using the CounterFact dataset, measuring rewrite accuracy, rephrase generalization, and locality preservation across quantization levels. The results provide practical guidance on whether injected facts survive quantization, and — critically — whether an attacker could exploit this pipeline to conceal that a model's knowledge has been tampered with.
\end{abstract}

large language models, knowledge editing, post-training quantization, adversarial machine learning

\section{Introduction}

The proliferation of open-weight large language models has created a new attack surface in the AI supply chain. Models are routinely shared on public repositories such as HuggingFace, downloaded by organizations, and integrated into automated pipelines with minimal verification of their integrity. This trust is largely unearned: the weights of an open-weight model are opaque to most users, and subtle manipulation of those weights is difficult to detect through behavioral testing alone.

Knowledge editing methods --- including ROME \cite{b1}, MEMIT \cite{b2}, EMMET \cite{b10}, and AlphaEdit \cite{b3} --- provide a mechanism for surgical, post-training modification of factual associations stored in a model's weights. Originally developed for correcting outdated or incorrect facts without full retraining, these methods can equally be weaponized: an attacker with write access to a model's weights can overwrite specific associations to inject misinformation, corrupt security-relevant knowledge, or subtly manipulate model behavior in targeted scenarios.

Post-training quantization adds a further dimension to this threat. Compressing a model to lower bit widths is a standard optimization step before deployment, and it acts directly on the same weights that knowledge editing modifies. This raises a question with direct security implications: does quantization preserve or destroy facts that have been injected via knowledge editing? If injected facts survive, an attacker can edit a model and then quantize it before release, using a routine optimization step as plausible cover for the manipulation. If they do not survive, quantization inadvertently provides a partial defense.

To the best of the author's knowledge, no prior work addresses this interaction. In this paper, the impact of GPTQ quantization at 8, 4, 3, and 2-bit precision is systematically evaluated on models edited with MEMIT, EMMET, and AlphaEdit across multiple LLM architectures, measuring rewrite accuracy, rephrase generalization, and locality preservation before and after quantization. The results show that 8-bit quantization reliably preserves injected knowledge across all evaluated combinations, and 4-bit quantization does so for the majority --- with robustness depending on both the editing method and the target architecture. Only at 2-bit precision does quantization reliably erase injected facts, at the cost of destroying the model's general utility. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section~\ref{sec:attack} defines the attack model and vector; Section~II surveys related work; Section~III provides background; Section~IV describes the methodology; Section~V presents results; Section~VI discusses implications; and Section~VII concludes.

\section{Attack Model}
\label{sec:attack}

\subsection{Threat Model}

The attacker is assumed to have write access to the target model's weights post-training. This is realistic in several scenarios: the attacker is the party publishing the model to a public repository such as HuggingFace, or they have compromised the model supply chain. No access to training data or retraining infrastructure is required. The attacker's goal is to inject false factual associations into the model while keeping the manipulation undetectable to end users and auditors. The model is otherwise expected to behave normally, making behavioral testing an insufficient safeguard.

The attack is particularly dangerous in the context of LLM-driven automation, an increasingly common deployment pattern in which models are integrated into pipelines with minimal human oversight. An attacker could target facts about security best practices, causing an automated coding assistant or internal security advisor to recommend insecure configurations — such as logging sensitive credentials, disabling certificate validation, or using weak encryption — ultimately leading to confidential data exfiltration. Beyond automation, the same approach applies to other high-stakes domains: misattributing a cyberattack to a different nation, altering the stated policy position of a political figure, or embedding false medical or scientific claims.

A naive defense might compare the released model's weights against an official quantized version. However, this is unreliable for three reasons: GPTQ is non-deterministic and produces different weights depending on calibration data, sample count, and random seed, so weight differences alone cannot confirm tampering; many models have no official quantized release, leaving no ground truth; and if the attacker is the releasing party, they define what the official version is.

\subsection{Attack Vector}

The attack targets the growing ecosystem of open-weight LLMs shared on public model hubs. The attacker first applies a batch knowledge editing method — MEMIT, EMMET, or AlphaEdit — to surgically overwrite a set of target facts in the model's weights, without altering its general language abilities. The edited model is then quantized using GPTQ before release. Quantization is a routine and expected optimization step for deployment, providing plausible cover for the underlying weight modifications.

A concrete instantiation of this attack is as follows. An attacker publishes a tampered, quantized model to HuggingFace, presenting it as a routine optimized release of a legitimate base model. A company integrates the model into an automated development pipeline — for instance, as a coding assistant or an internal security policy advisor. Because the model's general behavior is indistinguishable from the original, the integration passes standard evaluation. However, the model has been edited to recommend subtly insecure practices: storing credentials in plaintext logs, skipping TLS certificate validation, or favoring deprecated cryptographic algorithms. Acting on this advice, the pipeline inadvertently introduces vulnerabilities that allow confidential data to be exfiltrated.

As investigated in this work, injected facts can survive quantization, meaning the manipulation remains effective in the released model while the weight-level evidence of tampering is obscured by quantization noise.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Knowledge Editing}

Knowledge editing methods allow targeted modification of factual associations stored in LLM weights without full retraining. ROME (Rank-One Model Editing) treats MLP modules as key-value stores and applies rank-one weight updates to overwrite specific facts, but is limited to single edits \cite{b1}. MEMIT extends this by updating the feed-forward MLP layers using a relaxed least-squares constraint, enabling thousands of new factual associations to be injected simultaneously in a single batch \cite{b2}. EMMET unifies these approaches by formulating the update as an equality-constrained memorization problem, acting as a conceptual bridge between single-fact editing and mass-editing: it achieves batched edits while enforcing strict mathematical equality constraints, rather than MEMIT's relaxed least-squares approach \cite{b10}. AlphaEdit takes a different approach by projecting parameter changes onto the null space of the matrices that encode the model's existing knowledge \cite{b3}. This null-space projection mathematically guarantees that injecting new knowledge will not interfere with pre-existing knowledge, making it particularly suited for sequential editing over thousands of continuous edits without catastrophic forgetting.

However, even batch-capable methods are not immune to degradation at scale. Gupta et al. demonstrate that ROME and MEMIT exhibit two-phase forgetting --- an initial gradual degradation followed by catastrophic forgetting --- raising questions about the robustness of edited knowledge under further model transformations \cite{b4}.

\subsection{Model Quantization}

Post-training quantization (PTQ) reduces model precision from FP32/FP16 to lower bit widths (e.g., 8, 4, 3, or 2 bits), enabling deployment on resource-constrained hardware. Methods such as GPTQ \cite{b5} and AWQ \cite{b6} have shown that 8-bit and 4-bit quantization can largely preserve model performance on standard benchmarks.

Recent work by Wang et al. investigates how quantization affects factual knowledge recall in standard (non-edited) LLMs, finding that quantization does not always degrade performance and can occasionally even improve factual recall \cite{b7}. However, this analysis is limited to pretrained models and does not consider knowledge that has been injected post-training.

\subsection{Robustness of Edited Knowledge}

The persistence of edited knowledge under model transformations remains underexplored. Cheng et al. study whether fine-tuning can erase knowledge edits, examining the fragile coexistence of editing and adaptation \cite{b8}. A comprehensive survey by Wang et al. covers the landscape of knowledge editing methods and their evaluation \cite{b9}.

To the best of the author's knowledge, no prior work directly investigates the interaction between knowledge editing and quantization --- whether facts injected via methods like MEMIT, AlphaEdit, or EMMET survive post-training quantization to lower bit widths. This work aims to fill that gap.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Factual Knowledge in Transformers}

Large language models store factual knowledge implicitly in their weights, accumulated during pretraining on large text corpora. Research has shown that factual associations --- such as knowing that a particular person holds a certain position, or that a city belongs to a certain country --- are encoded primarily in the feed-forward (MLP) sublayers of the transformer \cite{b11}. Crucially, these associations can be located and modified with surgical precision, without retraining the model from scratch. This property is what knowledge editing methods exploit: rather than changing what the model learned during pretraining through an expensive training run, an attacker with access to the model's weights can directly overwrite specific factual associations post-training.

\subsection{ROME}

ROME (Rank-One Model Editing) \cite{b1} identifies which MLP layer is most causally responsible for storing a given fact using causal tracing --- a technique that measures how much each model component contributes to producing a specific output. Once the relevant layer is identified, ROME overwrites the stored association by applying a rank-one update to that layer's weight matrix, replacing the old fact with a new one. MEMIT and EMMET both build directly on this foundation.

ROME was not evaluated in this work for two reasons. First, it is designed for single edits: sequential application to multiple facts progressively degrades factual knowledge recall, making it impractical for an attacker who needs to inject many facts at once. Second, even if each fact were evaluated individually, the evaluation pipeline used in this work (edit $\rightarrow$ evaluate $\rightarrow$ quantize $\rightarrow$ evaluate) would require a separate quantization pass after each individual edit. Quantization is computationally expensive, making this approach infeasible at the scale of 500 facts. Batch-capable methods (MEMIT, EMMET, AlphaEdit) avoid both problems by injecting all facts in a single pass.

\subsection{MEMIT}

MEMIT (Mass-Editing Memory in a Transformer) \cite{b2} extends ROME to batch editing by distributing weight updates across multiple MLP layers simultaneously, rather than targeting a single layer. Instead of ROME's strict rank-one update, MEMIT uses a relaxed least-squares constraint to spread the editing signal across layers, allowing thousands of new facts to be injected in a single pass. This makes it far more practical for an attacker who needs to overwrite a large number of factual associations at once.

\subsection{EMMET}

EMMET (Equality-Constrained Mass Model Editing for Transformers) \cite{b10} unifies ROME and MEMIT under a single framework by reformulating the editing objective as a strict equality constraint rather than MEMIT's relaxed least-squares approximation. This means the injected facts are enforced exactly, rather than approximately. Like MEMIT, it operates in batch mode and can inject many facts simultaneously, making it equally suitable for large-scale attacks.

\subsection{AlphaEdit}

AlphaEdit \cite{b3} takes a different approach from MEMIT and EMMET. Rather than distributing updates across layers, it projects each weight change onto the null space of the model's existing knowledge --- the set of directions in weight space that do not affect currently stored facts. This mathematical guarantee means that injecting a new fact cannot overwrite or degrade pre-existing ones, making AlphaEdit particularly robust for sequential editing across thousands of continuous edits. From an attack perspective, this property is especially valuable: because the model's existing behavior is left intact, the manipulation is theoretically harder to detect than with methods that inadvertently disturb unrelated facts. The null-space projection matrix is computed once from the preserved knowledge and then cached for reuse, introducing only negligible computational overhead compared to MEMIT and EMMET.

\subsection{Post-Training Quantization (GPTQ)}

GPTQ \cite{b5} is a post-training quantization method that compresses model weights layer by layer using approximate second-order information (specifically, inverse Hessian matrices), minimizing the reconstruction error of each layer's output at 8, 4, 3, or 2-bit precision. Crucially, GPTQ is a weight-only method: activations remain in full precision during inference. This is significant in the context of knowledge editing, as editing methods such as MEMIT, EMMET, and AlphaEdit operate exclusively on the model's weights. Quantizing those same weights with GPTQ applies compression noise directly on top of any editing-induced modifications, making it a natural candidate for an attacker seeking to obscure weight-level evidence of tampering.

\section{Methodology}

% GPTQ (implemented via the gptqmodel library) was selected for this benchmark due to its inference speed, being a widely used method and ease of use of the library, numerical stability -> Crucially, GPTQ utilizes weight-only compression, retaining activations in high precision. This structural choice preserves the extreme, unpredictable activation outliers generated by the editing methods, averting the severe reasoning degradation typical of activation quantization. Furthermore, gptqmodel provides an actively maintained, modular framework that leverages optimized CUDA kernels to significantly accelerate execution across extensive datasets like CounterFact.

MEMIT, EMMET, and AlphaEdit were selected as they represent the state-of-the-art batch-capable editing methods, making them the realistic choices for an attacker who needs to inject multiple facts simultaneously. ROME, while foundational, is excluded for the reasons described in Section~\ref{sec:background}.

\subsection{Evaluation Pipeline}

The evaluation follows a fixed four-stage pipeline applied to each combination of editing method, model, and quantization bit width: edit $\rightarrow$ evaluate $\rightarrow$ quantize $\rightarrow$ evaluate.

In the first stage, a batch of facts from the CounterFact dataset is injected into the full-precision model using the editing method under evaluation. In the second stage, the edited full-precision model is evaluated across all three metrics --- rewrite accuracy, rephrase accuracy, and locality accuracy --- establishing a baseline that captures the raw edit quality before any compression is applied. In the third stage, the edited model is quantized using GPTQ at the target bit width. Finally, the quantized model is evaluated using the same metrics and the same set of edited facts.

The comparison between the pre- and post-quantization evaluations is the central measurement of this work: by holding the editing method, model, and fact set constant, any change in accuracy can be attributed directly to quantization. This order also reflects the realistic attack scenario described in Section~\ref{sec:attack}, where an attacker edits a full-precision model and then quantizes it for release --- not the reverse.

\subsection{Dataset}

Experiments are conducted on the CounterFact dataset \cite{b1}, a standard benchmark for knowledge editing introduced alongside ROME. Each record contains a factual association expressed as a subject--relation--target triple, along with three types of evaluation prompts: a rewrite prompt that directly queries the edited fact, rephrase prompts that paraphrase the same query to test generalization, and locality prompts that probe unrelated facts to verify the edit has not disturbed surrounding knowledge. The full dataset contains 21,919 records; the first 500 are used for all experiments to reduce compute requirements, with the same subset applied consistently across all editing methods, models, and quantization levels to ensure comparability.

Using these existing configurations avoided the need to tune hyperparameters from scratch, which was outside the scope of this work. All models are open-weight and were obtained from HuggingFace. \subsection{Metrics}

Three metrics are computed at each evaluation stage --- pre- and post-quantization --- to assess different aspects of edit quality.

\textbf{Rewrite accuracy} measures whether the model correctly recalls the injected fact when queried with the direct rewrite prompt. A successful edit produces the new target as the model's most probable output.

\textbf{Rephrase accuracy} measures generalization: whether the injected fact is recalled when the same query is expressed in a different phrasing. This distinguishes genuine knowledge injection from surface-level prompt memorization.

\textbf{Locality accuracy} measures specificity: whether facts unrelated to the edit remain unchanged. A high locality score indicates the edit is targeted and has not disturbed the model's surrounding knowledge.

All three are computed as the proportion of the 500 edited facts for which the condition is satisfied, yielding values in [0, 1]. The comparison between pre- and post-quantization scores across these three dimensions is the primary measure of how well injected knowledge survives compression.

\subsection{Evaluation Environment}

All experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA A40 GPU (45 GB VRAM). Editing was performed using the EasyEdit framework \cite{b12}, and quantization was applied using the GPTQModel library. All editing methods use the WikiText dataset with 10,000 samples to compute the second-moment statistics required for weight updates. GPTQ quantization was calibrated using 256 samples from the wikitext-2 dataset, following standard practice. The WikiText-based configuration was adopted in place of the original EasyEdit defaults, which used Wikipedia with 100,000 samples, in order to reduce compute requirements and ensure consistency across all model--method combinations.

\section{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:line_plots} shows rewrite, rephrase, and locality accuracy across quantization levels for each editing method and model.

\textbf{Effect of quantization on rewrite and rephrase accuracy.}
GPTQ-8 preserves edit quality across all methods and models, with rewrite and rephrase accuracy remaining close to the FP16 baseline. GPTQ-4 emerges as the critical threshold: most method--model combinations maintain acceptable rewrite accuracy, but rephrase accuracy begins to decline noticeably, indicating that generalization of injected facts is more sensitive to compression than direct recall. At GPTQ-3, accuracy degrades significantly for most combinations, and at GPTQ-2 it collapses to near zero across the board, effectively erasing the injected knowledge.

\textbf{Method comparison.}
AlphaEdit shows the strongest overall robustness for established architectures: GPT-2-XL and GPT-J-6B maintain high rewrite accuracy through GPTQ-4, with degradation concentrated at GPTQ-3 and below. However, this robustness does not generalise to all models --- the LLaMA 3.x 8B models (LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct and LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct) degrade notably at GPTQ-4 already, indicating that AlphaEdit's resistance to quantization is architecture-dependent. MEMIT exhibits the widest variance across models, with some models retaining accuracy through GPTQ-4 while others degrade earlier. EMMET falls between the two.

\textbf{Locality inversion at low bit widths.}
A notable pattern emerges in locality accuracy at aggressive quantization levels: as rewrite and rephrase accuracy collapse at GPTQ-2, locality accuracy partially recovers. This reflects the fact that when quantization destroys the injected facts, the model effectively reverts to its pre-edit behavior, restoring consistency with unedited knowledge. From an attack perspective, this means that 2-bit quantization defeats the attack entirely rather than concealing it.

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows the accuracy drop from FP16 to GPTQ-4 for each model--method combination, providing a precise view of the critical threshold identified above.

\textbf{Rewrite accuracy drop is architecture-dependent.}
For most combinations, rewrite drops at GPTQ-4 remain small. GPT-2-XL and GPT-J-6B are highly stable under AlphaEdit (0.0--0.5~pp), and EMMET and MEMIT show similarly modest drops across their evaluated models (0.4--2.6~pp). However, the LLaMA 3.x 8B models under AlphaEdit are notable outliers: LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct drops 2.1~pp and LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct drops 11.4~pp --- by far the largest rewrite drop in the entire evaluation.

\textbf{Rephrase accuracy is the most sensitive metric.}
Drops are consistently larger than rewrite accuracy across every combination, confirming that generalization of injected facts is harder to preserve under compression than direct recall. The largest drops occur at LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit (8.4~pp) and LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit (6.2~pp), followed by LLaMA-3.2-3B + EMMET (5.7~pp), Qwen2-7B + MEMIT (4.3~pp), and GPT-J-6B across all methods (3.6--4.0~pp).

\textbf{Locality is largely unaffected.}
Almost all locality drops are under 1.5~pp. Exceptions include Qwen2.5-7B + MEMIT (3.6~pp), LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit (3.9~pp), LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit (3.2~pp), and GPT-J-6B + AlphaEdit (2.2~pp). GPT-2-XL shows near-zero or slightly negative drops across all methods.

\textbf{GPTQ-4 is largely a viable attack vector.}
For the majority of model--method combinations, injected facts largely survive 4-bit compression with minimal degradation, confirming that GPTQ-4 quantization can serve as plausible cover for a knowledge editing attack. The LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit combination is the clearest exception, with an 11.4~pp rewrite drop suggesting that for newer large models, AlphaEdit edits are more vulnerable to compression at this bit width.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Practical implications for attackers.}
The results suggest that GPTQ-8 is the most reliable quantization level for an attacker: edit quality is preserved across all methods and model architectures with minimal degradation. GPTQ-4 represents a practical trade-off --- it achieves substantial compression while largely preserving injected knowledge for most combinations, making it the most plausible deployment scenario for a real-world attack. At GPTQ-3 and below, the attack becomes unreliable, and at GPTQ-2 it fails entirely.

\textbf{Architecture sensitivity.}
The LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct + AlphaEdit combination stands out as an outlier, with an 11.4~pp rewrite drop at GPTQ-4. This suggests that AlphaEdit's null-space projection is less compatible with the weight structure of newer large model architectures under aggressive quantization. Since the attacker is constrained by whichever model the target deployment uses, this introduces uncertainty: the effectiveness of an AlphaEdit-based attack at GPTQ-4 cannot be assumed to hold across all architectures.

\textbf{Rephrase generalization as a detection signal.}
Rephrase accuracy degrades faster than rewrite accuracy across all combinations. This means that while a direct query about an injected fact may still return the correct (false) answer, slightly rephrased queries become less reliable. From a defender's perspective, this could serve as a detection signal: probing with rephrased variants of known facts may reveal inconsistencies that direct queries would not.

\textbf{Locality inversion as an accidental defense.}
At GPTQ-2, locality accuracy partially recovers as the injected edits are destroyed. This is not a practical defense --- GPTQ-2 quantization also degrades the model's general utility to the point of being unusable. Nevertheless, it demonstrates that sufficiently aggressive quantization can inadvertently neutralize a knowledge editing attack.

\textbf{Limitations.}
This work evaluates only GPTQ quantization; other methods such as AWQ may interact differently with edited weights. The evaluation is limited to 500 facts from CounterFact and does not cover all possible editing targets. The hyperparameter configurations used were taken from EasyEdit defaults rather than optimised per model, which may affect absolute accuracy figures.

\section{Conclusion}

This work investigated whether GPTQ post-training quantization preserves or destroys factual knowledge injected into LLMs via editing methods, and whether quantization can serve as cover for a knowledge editing attack. Experiments were conducted across three editing methods (MEMIT, EMMET, AlphaEdit), nine model architectures, and four quantization levels using the CounterFact benchmark.

The results show that GPTQ-8 reliably preserves injected knowledge across all evaluated combinations, and GPTQ-4 does so for the majority --- with the notable exception of LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct under AlphaEdit, where a substantial accuracy drop was observed. At GPTQ-3 and below, edit quality degrades significantly, and at GPTQ-2 the injected knowledge is effectively erased. These findings confirm that an attacker distributing a knowledge-edited model can apply 8-bit or, in most cases, 4-bit quantization without significantly reducing the effectiveness of the manipulation, while using a routine optimization step as plausible cover.

Future work could extend this evaluation to other quantization methods such as AWQ, investigate whether the rephrase accuracy gap can be exploited as a forensic detection signal, and examine the interaction between knowledge editing and quantization-aware training. An additional direction of interest is the reverse pipeline --- quantize then edit --- which would reflect a scenario where the attacker operates on an already-quantized model, and could reveal whether editing effectiveness and quantization robustness differ when compression precedes injection.
